Nuclear War: The Rising Risk, and How We Stop It

Thinking about nuclear war, especially in this day and age, is completely rational. The idea of war has been a constant and consistent concept in human history, as it is the final form or show of dominance over an opposing group of people. The world is built on the concept of dominance and conquest, we unfortunately follow in these footsteps everyday, from the smallest of things like winning a game to hitting a winning auction. War is something that isn’t too far behind those same things we are so closely tied to on a daily basis. In agreement with the looming threat of humanity and its lust for war, the article, Nuclear War: The Rising Risk, and How We Stop It, centers on the idea that we now have weapons of mass destruction that are more easily attainable and widespread. The chance for war is higher than it’s been in the past and the amount of power amassed by different countries has also increased.

The New York Times makes a great statement article by creating a visual story that imbues the fear and catastrophe of the chance and actuality of war. Using these nuclear warheads that nine countries have easy access to now are drawn out and illustrated in a scrolling story of destruction. We read about the danger and apparent closeness we have to nuclear war, as well as the repercussions of a bomb being dropped and how it would impact the environment and people. What if there’s retaliation? What happens to the people around the world, and not just in or near the blast radius? The entirety of this article pertains to the ‘what-ifs’ and just how doomed we’d be if a nuclear bomb were to be dropped. It’s an unfortunate scenario, however it looms around every single person around the world, due to our higher powers having more control over our lives than we do.
